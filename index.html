The Recycle Park project was a collaboration between the Reality Computing students and the Architecture students in UDBS. Recycle Park is a park in Pittsburgh that is being renovated with the help of UDBS. Our role in this process was to use virtual reality to help the community take an active role in the renovation by allowing them to help plan the new park by allowing them to move the pieces around the park model and, in real-time, see what it would look like through the VR goggles.

Pictured here is a sample of the April Tags used. Beneath each group of four is a family name and for our project we used the 36h11 family.

Although these are different sizes, this shows the difference between the stock April Tags on the right and final tags we used on the right. As you can see the id of the tag was moved and made smaller and a lot of white space was removed also in order to make the tags as small as possible. The size of the tags was important because they had to be big enough to be seen by the cameras and also small enough that they would fit on the bottom of the models.

This shows a closer look at the tracking of the tags. If you look closely at the edges of this image, you can see the distortion of the picture. This is because we originally inteded to use a single camera with a fish eye lense in order to be able to see the entire park model but this screenshot was taken when the fish eye lense wasn't used meaning the camera adjustment was off because it was intended for a different camera.

This shows the calibration process for the camera. The checkerboard was used with the OpenCV calibration program. The calibration program outputs an camera matrix and distorion coefficients which are used to adjust the camera.

In order to communicate the position of the tags to the virtual reality computer, we used websockets and a node server. The websockets were used to send the id of the tag, the position and the orientation. The top image is an image of the data when it is received by the server and the bottom is an image of the data being sent.

Digital models of the individual pieces were placed inside the point cloud of the park in virtual reality. This allowed the wearer of the goggles to see what the park would look like with the individual pieces in the places they are put on the physcial model. In order for the digital pieces to be orientated in the same way of the physical models, we placed a digital plane on the models to represent the tag. This plane had the same specifications as the physical tags.
